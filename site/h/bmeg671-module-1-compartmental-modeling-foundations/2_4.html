2.4 Central and peripheral lumping
The transcript also gives a pharmacokinetic example: blood and highly perfused
organs may be lumped into a central compartment, while slower tissues become
peripheral compartments [6]. The point is not that liver, kidney, lung, brain,
and blood are physically one place. The point is that, at the time scale and
measurement resolution of the experiment, they may equilibrate quickly relative
to other transfers.

Lumping is powerful, but it is also dangerous. It hides spatial detail. A
central compartment volume is not a literal anatomical volume; it is an apparent
volume compatible with the observed concentration-time curve. A useful rule
is:
lump states only when the lost detail is not needed for the question
of interest.
This phrase anticipates ICH M15: model quality is judged relative to the ques-
tion the model is meant to answer, not in the abstract [3].
