This second part asks what the equations imply. Once a model is written, the
central questions change:
– Can the hidden states be inferred from the measured outputs?
– Can the input actually move the system in the directions we care about?
– Near a biological operating point, can a nonlinear system be approximated
by a linear one?
– Which steady states are stable, unstable, or biologically irrelevant?
– Does the model preserve nonnegativity of concentrations, cell counts, and
viral loads?
– Are oscillations real attracting cycles or merely initial-condition-dependent
closed orbits?
– When a dose is given repeatedly or piecewise, should it be encoded as a
reaction, an input, a dose object, or a segmented simulation?
The lectures answer these questions in a deliberately practical order. They
start with SimBiology because a graphical model builder forces us to name
species, reactions, parameters, units, and dose objects. They then return to
matrix and nonlinear analysis because no graphical tool can replace the math-
ematical questions of observability, controllability, stationary points, stability,
and invariance.
