(iii) When should log trapezoidal interpolation be preferred over lin-
ear trapezoidal interpolation?
Log trapezoidal interpolation is preferred over clearly declining positive
concentration intervals when the decline is approximately exponential. In
that setting, the curve is closer to a straight line on the log-concentration
scale than on the concentration scale, and the linear trapezoid tends to
overestimate area because the chord sits above a convex exponential de-
cline. It should not be used across rising intervals, zero values, negative
baseline-corrected concentrations, or BLQ values treated as zero. This is
why many workows use linear-up/log-down: linear during absorption or
rising portions, log during post-peak decline.

(viii) Why does BE use geometric mean ratios and 90% condence
intervals?
AUC and C
max
are positive, multiplicative, often right-skewed quantities,
so BE analysis usually works on the log scale. On the log scale, treat-
ment dierences become dierences in means; after exponentiation, they
become ratios of geometric means. This matches the scientic question:
not whether test and reference dier by a xed concentration amount, but
whether exposure is acceptably close in relative terms.
The 90% condence interval comes from the two one-sided tests logic. To
show average BE, we need enough evidence that the test/reference ratio is
not too low and not too high. For many products, this means the 90% CI
for the geometric mean ratio lies within 0.80 to 1.25. The interval is stricter
than simply asking whether the point estimate is near 1: both uncertainty
and bias matter.
(ix) What assumptions make non-parametric superposition reason-
able?
Non-parametric superposition treats the single-dose concentration prole as
a response kernel and stacks shifted copies of it across repeated doses. This
is reasonable when PK is linear and time-invariant: dose proportionality
holds, clearance and distribution do not change over time, absorption behav-
ior is reproducible, and one dose does not alter the disposition of the next
dose except through additive residual concentrations. It becomes fragile
with saturable metabolism, autoinduction, enzyme inhibition, time-varying
disease, formulation changes, nonlinear protein binding, target-mediated
disposition, or circadian/input changes that make each dose behave dier-
ently.
(x) When should I stop using NCA alone and move to a compart-
mental or population model?
Stop using NCA alone when the question needs a mechanism, prediction,
covariate explanation, sparse-data borrowing, or simulation. Examples in-
clude optimizing untested dosing regimens, estimating individual exposure
from sparse clinical samples, separating absorption from elimination, ex-
plaining food or formulation eects, modeling accumulation under non-
linear clearance, linking exposure to response, handling time-varying co-
variates, studying special populations, or making pediatric/renal/hepatic-
impairment predictions. NCA is excellent for summarizing observed curves;
compartmental and population models are needed when we must explain
the curve, borrow information across subjects, and predict outside the ob-
served design.
